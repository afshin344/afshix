A personalized gift‑discovery platform, built for an affiliate model

Live Platform giftpals.com ↗

Giftpals launched as a SaaS gift‑discovery website based on the founder's market insight: people don't want to browse a catalog, they want to be handed a short, relevant list for a specific person and occasion.

the problem

Too many gifting filters, not enough of a shortcut

The core challenge was building tools and flows that helped a user land on a personalized gift idea fast — filtered by parameters like the recipient's profession, gender, age group, and interests — without turning the experience into a generic e‑commerce filter panel. The business model added a second constraint: the interface needed to support affiliate‑driven monetization without feeling like an ad‑stuffed catalog.

process

Borrowing mental models people already trust

Rather than inventing a new interaction pattern, I leaned on UX patterns users already understand from social platforms and retail — recognizable filtering, shareable lists, discovery feeds — so the "personalized gift finder" concept didn't need to be explained before it could be used.

I built the user flows and workflow diagrams for discovery by recipient (profession, age, interests) and by occasion, then presented progress and prototypes directly to the founder and stakeholders to keep design decisions aligned with the business goals driving the roadmap.

key decisions

Where the trade‑offs actually happened

decision — social patterns over retail patterns

Combining engaging interface design with UX patterns common on social platforms — sharing, following, list‑building — encouraged community‑driven content and repeat visits, instead of a one‑off transactional filter search.

decision — usability audit before scaling features

Rather than continuing to add discovery filters, I ran a usability audit on the existing flow first. The redesign that followed reduced usability testing errors by 20% — evidence that the fix was in flow clarity, not feature count.

the solution

Discovery that feels like browsing, not filtering

The final experience lets users discover and share gift ideas in a way that mirrors browsing a social feed more than querying a database — while every result still routes through the affiliate model funding the business.

reflection

What I'd revisit

Leaning on social‑platform patterns made the concept easy to grasp fast, but it also means the discovery feed competes for the same attention span as the platforms it borrowed from — worth a longer‑term look at whether that's a strength or a ceiling on engagement.
