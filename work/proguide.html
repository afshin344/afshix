Turning prostate‑cancer research into a patient‑facing MVP

the problem

Patients arrive at a diagnosis with almost no usable information — and clinics don't have time to fill the gap.

Prostate cancer is one of the most common cancers affecting men, yet most patients enter the screening or treatment conversation without a real understanding of their options. The research team wanted a tool patients could use in the waiting room, before a biopsy, or after a diagnosis — something that made a dense, clinical topic navigable without oversimplifying it.

The constraint that shaped everything: this had to be trustworthy enough for a medical institution to put its name on, and simple enough for a patient with zero clinical background to use under stress.

process

Reviewing patient and physician scenarios before touching UI

I worked from real patient and physician scenarios provided by the clinical team to map where people actually get stuck — not where a generic health app assumes they get stuck. That meant separating two very different needs: a patient trying to understand a new diagnosis, and a physician who needed the tool to reinforce (not replace) the conversation happening in the room.

From there I built the information architecture around personalized, categorized health information — risk factors, screening guidance, treatment options — so each user only sees what's relevant to where they are in the journey, rather than a static wall of medical content.

key decisions

Where the trade‑offs actually happened

decision — personalization vs. simplicity

Risk calculators and screening‑eligibility quizzes tailor content to the individual's health profile, but every extra question risks losing an anxious user. I scoped personalization to the handful of inputs (age, family history, symptoms) that meaningfully change the guidance shown, rather than modeling the full clinical picture.

decision — returning‑user continuity

Spacer / Return capability built into standard profiles. Patients often research a diagnosis over weeks, not one sitting. The app remembers where a registered user left off, so a return visit picks up the thread instead of restarting a stressful research process from zero.

decision — bilingual from day one

Given the equity goal of the research team, English and Spanish were built in from the start rather than bolted on — which shaped how content modules were structured so translation didn't break layout or meaning.

the solution

A guided, categorized information tool — not a static brochure

Working closely with the development team, I ensured accurate implementation of the UI and user flows across the core processes: understanding risk, preparing for screening, and exploring treatment options if diagnosed. The result is a tool patients can use anytime — including while waiting in a clinic — to arrive at their appointment with more focused, specific questions.

reflection

What I'd revisit

The personalization inputs (age, family history, symptoms) were scoped deliberately narrow to protect simplicity — but as the platform gathers real usage data, some of that risk‑calculator logic likely needs to get smarter without adding steps to the flow. That's the next trade‑off to test, not something I'd call solved.
